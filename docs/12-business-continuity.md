# Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery

## Overview

Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery (BCDR) are organizational
capabilities designed to ensure that critical business functions can
continue operating during and after disruptive events.

While security controls focus on preventing incidents, Business
Continuity and Disaster Recovery focus on maintaining operations and
recovering from disruptions.

Organizations must assume that failures, disasters, cyberattacks, and
unexpected events will eventually occur.

The objective is not only to survive disruptions, but to recover
efficiently and continue delivering critical services.

---

## Why It Matters

Disruptions can affect an organization's ability to operate.

Examples include:

- Cyberattacks
- Ransomware incidents
- Infrastructure failures
- Cloud service outages
- Human errors
- Natural disasters
- Supply chain disruptions

Without effective continuity planning, organizations may experience:

- Extended downtime
- Financial losses
- Customer impact
- Regulatory consequences
- Reputational damage

---

## Business Continuity vs Disaster Recovery

Although closely related, these concepts serve different purposes.

### Business Continuity

Business Continuity focuses on maintaining critical business functions
during disruptions.

Key objective:

Continue operating despite adverse conditions.

Examples:

- Alternative business processes
- Remote work procedures
- Manual operations
- Alternative suppliers

### Disaster Recovery

Disaster Recovery focuses on restoring systems, infrastructure, and
technology services after disruption.

Key objective:

Restore normal operations as quickly as possible.

Examples:

- System restoration
- Infrastructure recovery
- Data recovery
- Service restoration

---

## Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

Business Continuity planning often begins with a Business Impact Analysis.

A BIA helps organizations identify:

- Critical business processes
- Dependencies
- Potential impacts
- Recovery priorities

Key questions include:

- Which processes are most important?
- What happens if they become unavailable?
- How long can they remain unavailable?
- What resources are required?

---

## Recovery Objectives

Organizations commonly define recovery objectives to guide planning.

### Recovery Time Objective (RTO)

The maximum acceptable downtime.

Example:

A critical application may require restoration within two hours.

### Recovery Point Objective (RPO)

The maximum acceptable amount of data loss.

Example:

An organization may accept losing up to one hour of data.

These objectives should be based on business requirements.

---

## Components of a Business Continuity Program

A mature continuity program typically includes several components.

### Risk Assessment

Identify threats that could disrupt operations.

Examples:

- Cybersecurity incidents
- Infrastructure failures
- Environmental events

### Business Impact Analysis

Determine operational impacts and priorities.

### Continuity Strategies

Define methods for maintaining operations.

Examples:

- Alternate work locations
- Remote work capabilities
- Redundant services

### Recovery Planning

Document recovery procedures.

### Testing and Exercises

Validate continuity and recovery capabilities.

### Continuous Improvement

Review and update plans regularly.

---

## Disaster Recovery Strategies

Organizations may use multiple recovery approaches.

### Backup Restoration

Recover data and systems from backups.

### High Availability

Reduce downtime through redundant systems.

### Geographic Redundancy

Deploy resources across multiple locations.

### Cloud Recovery

Leverage cloud services to support restoration efforts.

### Infrastructure Rebuild

Recreate environments using documented configurations and automation.

---

## The Role of Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity and Business Continuity are closely connected.

Security incidents often trigger continuity and recovery activities.

Examples include:

- Ransomware attacks
- Data destruction
- Service disruption attacks
- Critical system compromise

Organizations should ensure that continuity plans address cybersecurity
scenarios.

---

## Testing and Validation

A plan that has never been tested should not be assumed to work.

Organizations should conduct:

### Tabletop Exercises

Discussion-based simulations.

### Recovery Testing

Validation of recovery procedures.

### Failover Testing

Testing redundant infrastructure.

### Crisis Management Exercises

Testing organizational coordination and communication.

Testing helps identify weaknesses before real incidents occur.

---

## Relationship to Other Security Principles

Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery support multiple security
principles.

### Availability

Continuity planning directly supports availability.

### Incident Response

Recovery activities often follow incident response efforts.

### Backup and Recovery

Recovery capabilities depend on reliable backups.

### Risk Management

Continuity planning reduces organizational risk.

### Defense in Depth

Recovery capabilities provide resilience when preventive controls fail.

---

## Real-World Example

Consider a ransomware attack affecting an organization's primary data
center.

### Event

Critical systems become unavailable.

### Business Continuity Response

Essential business processes continue through alternative procedures.

### Disaster Recovery Response

Systems are restored in a secondary environment.

### Outcome

Operations continue with limited disruption.

Without continuity planning, the organization could experience
significant operational and financial impacts.

---

## Common Mistakes

### Focusing Only on Technology

Business Continuity involves people, processes, and technology.

### Not Defining Recovery Objectives

RTO and RPO should be documented.

### Never Testing Plans

Untested plans create uncertainty.

### Ignoring Third-Party Dependencies

External providers can become single points of failure.

### Failing to Update Plans

Business environments change over time.

### Assuming Cloud Services Eliminate Continuity Requirements

Organizations remain responsible for continuity planning.

---

## Benefits

Effective BCDR programs provide:

- Reduced downtime
- Improved resilience
- Faster recovery
- Better risk management
- Improved stakeholder confidence
- Enhanced operational stability
- Stronger incident preparedness

---

## Key Takeaways

- Business Continuity focuses on maintaining operations.
- Disaster Recovery focuses on restoring technology services.
- Recovery objectives should align with business requirements.
- Business Impact Analysis helps determine priorities.
- Testing is essential.
- Cybersecurity incidents should be included in continuity planning.
- Resilience requires preparation before disruptions occur.

---

## Security Review Checklist

When evaluating an organization, consider the following questions:

### Planning

- Is a Business Continuity Plan documented?
- Is a Disaster Recovery Plan documented?
- Are plans reviewed regularly?

### Risk Assessment

- Have disruption scenarios been identified?
- Have critical dependencies been evaluated?

### Recovery Objectives

- Are RTO values defined?
- Are RPO values defined?
- Do recovery capabilities meet requirements?

### Testing

- Are plans tested regularly?
- Are recovery exercises performed?
- Are lessons learned documented?

### Resilience

- Are alternate operating procedures available?
- Is geographic redundancy implemented?
- Are third-party risks considered?
