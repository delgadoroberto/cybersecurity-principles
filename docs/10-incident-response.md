# Incident Response

## Overview

Incident Response is the structured process used to identify, contain,
eradicate, recover from, and learn from cybersecurity incidents.

Despite preventive controls, no organization can completely eliminate
risk.

Organizations must be prepared to respond effectively when security
incidents occur.

A mature Incident Response capability reduces business impact, limits
damage, accelerates recovery, and improves organizational resilience.

---

## Why It Matters

Security incidents can result in:

- Data breaches
- Service disruptions
- Financial losses
- Regulatory consequences
- Reputational damage
- Operational impacts

The speed and effectiveness of an organization's response often
determine the overall impact of an incident.

Effective Incident Response minimizes disruption and supports
business continuity.

---

## What Is a Security Incident?

A security incident is an event that threatens the confidentiality,
integrity, or availability of information systems or data.

Examples include:

- Unauthorized access
- Malware infections
- Ransomware attacks
- Data breaches
- Privilege escalation
- Insider threats
- Denial-of-service attacks
- Cloud misconfigurations resulting in exposure

Not every security event is an incident.

Incident Response processes help organizations determine when an event
requires formal response actions.

---

## Incident Response Lifecycle

Many organizations follow a structured Incident Response lifecycle.

### 1. Preparation

Preparation establishes the foundation for effective response.

Examples include:

- Incident Response plans
- Security tools
- Monitoring capabilities
- Team training
- Communication procedures
- Backup strategies

Preparation is often the most important phase.

Organizations that prepare effectively typically respond more efficiently.

### 2. Identification

Identify potential incidents and determine their nature and scope.

Examples:

- Security alerts
- User reports
- Monitoring events
- Threat intelligence

Key questions include:

- What happened?
- When did it happen?
- Which systems are affected?
- What is the potential impact?

### 3. Containment

Containment limits further damage.

Examples:

- Isolating systems
- Blocking malicious traffic
- Disabling compromised accounts
- Restricting access

The objective is to prevent the incident from spreading.

### 4. Eradication

Remove the root cause of the incident.

Examples:

- Removing malware
- Eliminating unauthorized access
- Correcting misconfigurations
- Applying security patches

### 5. Recovery

Restore systems and services to normal operation.

Examples:

- Restoring backups
- Rebuilding systems
- Validating functionality
- Monitoring for recurring activity

Recovery should be carefully controlled and verified.

### 6. Lessons Learned

Analyze the incident and identify opportunities for improvement.

Examples:

- Root cause analysis
- Control improvements
- Process enhancements
- Additional training

Organizations should use incidents as learning opportunities.

## Incident Classification

Not all incidents have the same severity.

Organizations often classify incidents based on:

### Impact

What is the potential business impact?

### Scope

How many systems or users are affected?

### Sensitivity

Does the incident involve sensitive data?

### Urgency

How quickly must action be taken?

Classification helps prioritize response efforts.

## Roles and Responsibilities

Effective Incident Response requires clearly defined responsibilities.

Examples may include:

### Security Team

- Investigation
- Containment
- Technical analysis

### IT Operations

- System recovery
- Infrastructure support
- Change implementation

### Management

- Decision-making
- Resource allocation
- Escalation support

### Legal and Compliance

- Regulatory considerations
- Notification requirements

### Communications

- Internal communications
- External communications
- Customer notifications

Roles should be defined before incidents occur.

## Evidence Handling

Incident investigations frequently rely on evidence.

Examples include:

- System logs
- Security alerts
- Network data
- Endpoint artifacts
- Cloud activity records

Evidence should be:

- Preserved
- Protected
- Documented
- Collected consistently

Proper evidence handling supports investigations and legal requirements.

## Common Incident Types

Organizations commonly encounter incidents such as:

### Malware

Malicious software affecting systems or users.

### Ransomware

Malware designed to encrypt data and disrupt operations.

### Credential Compromise

Unauthorized use of user credentials.

### Data Breach

Unauthorized access to sensitive information.

### Insider Threat

Actions performed by trusted individuals that create risk.

### Cloud Security Incident

Misconfigurations, unauthorized access, or exposure of cloud resources.

## Relationship to Other Security Principles

Incident Response depends on multiple security capabilities.

### Logging and Monitoring

Detection typically begins with visibility and alerting.

### Vulnerability Management

Unpatched vulnerabilities may contribute to incidents.

### Identity and Access Management

Compromised identities are a common attack vector.

### Backup and Recovery

Recovery often depends on reliable backups.

### Risk Management

Response priorities should align with business risk.

---

## Real-World Example

Consider a ransomware attack affecting critical servers.

### Identification

Security monitoring detects suspicious encryption activity.

### Containment

Affected systems are isolated from the network.

### Eradication

Malicious software is removed and compromised access is revoked.

### Recovery

Systems are restored from validated backups.

### Lessons Learned

Security controls are improved to reduce future risk.

The organization returns to normal operations with reduced impact.

---

## Common Mistakes

### Lack of Preparation

Organizations often focus on prevention while neglecting response planning.

### Undefined Roles

Confusion during incidents can delay response actions.

### Poor Communication

Lack of communication can increase business impact.

### Delayed Containment

Slow containment may allow incidents to spread.

### Inadequate Documentation

Insufficient records make investigations more difficult.

### Failure to Learn

Organizations should improve after every incident.

## Security Review Checklist

When evaluating a system or organization, consider the following questions:

### Preparation

- Is an Incident Response plan documented?
- Are roles and responsibilities defined?
- Are personnel trained?

### Detection

- Can incidents be identified quickly?
- Are monitoring capabilities in place?
- Are alerts reviewed?

### Containment

- Can compromised systems be isolated?
- Are emergency procedures defined?

### Recovery

- Can systems be restored?
- Have recovery procedures been tested?
- Are backups available?

### Improvement

- Are incidents documented?
- Is root cause analysis performed?
- Are lessons learned incorporated?
